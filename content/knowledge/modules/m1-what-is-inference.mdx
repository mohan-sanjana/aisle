Whenever an organization decides to invest in AI infrastructure, the request to IT teams is likely a vague one: "stand up some GPUs for the AI team"[cite: 1]. However, unlike traditional IT workloads, running the exact same model for a background RAG application is vastly different from running it for an interactive, low-latency chat experience. In this age of AI, those IT teams that can best collaborate with the AI workload owners are likely to be the most successful. Get it wrong, and you can spend a million dollars on infrastructure that does the wrong job[cite: 1].

## Two activities, very different infrastructure

Modern AI systems are not only fundamentally different from traditional IT workloads, but depending on the use case, they can look completely different from an infrastructure perspective.

<GlossaryTerm id="training">Training</GlossaryTerm> is the process of teaching a model. You take a vast dataset, often the entire public internet, and run gradient descent over it for weeks or months on thousands of GPUs. Training a model the size of Llama 3.1 70B takes on the order of a million GPU-hours. It is the most expensive thing humans do with computers, and it happens once per major model release. Almost no enterprise trains models from scratch. The few that do operate at the frontier (Anthropic, OpenAI, Meta, Google), and they run infrastructure measured in *gigawatts*, not megawatts.

<GlossaryTerm id="inference">Inference</GlossaryTerm> is what happens every time you actually *use* the model. Every chat message, every code completion, every document summary. The model's weights are fixed; you feed it some input, and it produces output. A single inference takes milliseconds to seconds, not weeks. But unlike training, you do it millions of times a day.

The analogy that helps most people see the difference: **training is like writing a book, and inference is reading it aloud**. 

## The AI workload landscape (and how it fits the library)

To understand where your infrastructure resources will actually go, it helps to stretch that book analogy across the five recognized categories of AI workloads, pairing the concept with the technical reality:

- **Training**
  - *The Analogy:* Writing the book from scratch. Authors sitting in a room for months, consuming massive amounts of raw energy and data.
  - *The Technical Truth:* Building a model from scratch by running weeks or months of compute on a frontier cluster.
- **Fine-Tuning**
  - *The Analogy:* Adding a custom appendix. Taking an existing book and spending a few days writing a specialized corporate chapter so it understands your company's terminology.
  - *The Technical Truth:* Specializing a pre-trained model on a smaller, custom dataset over hours or days on a much smaller cluster.
- **Inference**
  - *The Analogy:* Reading the book aloud. The main event, where a user asks a question and the system reads the book aloud to give them an answer.
  - *The Technical Truth:* Running the frozen model against real requests, delivering responses in milliseconds per request across potentially millions of queries a day.
- **Embeddings**
  - *The Analogy:* The card catalog. Translating raw text or data into a highly organized numerical index so the system knows exactly where to look later.
  - *The Technical Truth:* A special kind of lightweight inference that turns text or images into mathematical vectors for search.
- **Retrieval / RAG**
  - *The Analogy:* Consulting the reference desk. Pulling a relevant file from your company's secure filing cabinet and placing it right in front of the reader *while* they are reading.
  - *The Technical Truth:* Searching a vector database on every request to inject relevant, real-time context directly into the prompt.

Here is the vital takeaway: **Inference is where the actual business value lives.** Training, fine-tuning, embeddings, and retrieval are all ultimately just in service of a model making a decision or generating text in real-time. For the vast majority of enterprise on-prem deployments, running model inference—to securely extract insights from proprietary data or run real-time analysis—is the core objective.

## Why on-prem, why now

For a few years, "use the API" was a complete answer. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google offer hosted inference; tokens come out of the wire, your data goes in. For prototypes and many production workloads, that's still the right answer.

On-prem inference exists for a small number of concrete reasons, usually two or three at once:

- **Data residency.** Healthcare, finance, defense, and EU public-sector workloads often cannot send prompts to an external cloud API. Inference must happen strictly inside the organization's boundary.
- **Cost at scale.** Once you're past a few billion tokens a month, owned infrastructure becomes cheaper than API-billed inference. The crossover depends on your model and utilization, but the rule of thumb is roughly $1–2M per year of API spend.
- **Proprietary and Fine-Tuned Models.** If your data science team has heavily customized, fine-tuned, or optimized an open-weight model specifically for your business logic, hosting that highly proprietary IP on a third-party public API is often a non-starter.
- **Latency and locality.** Agentic workflows that make twenty tool calls per turn want millisecond round-trips. The closest cloud region is sometimes too far.

## Why inference breaks traditional capacity planning

Here's the surprise: inference looks deceptively simple from the outside. There's a model. You send it a prompt. It sends back a response. You might assume capacity planning means stacking up enough compute to handle the request volume, the same way you size a database for queries per second (QPS).

It doesn't work that way. In the AI world, the initial choices made by the data science team—the specific model, the precision (quantization), and the maximum context length—do not just dictate how many GPUs you need. They practically define the design of your entire infrastructure stack.

A shift in model size can instantly dictate whether you need high-speed, intra-node fabric like NVLink, or standard PCIe slots. A longer context length requirement can drastically alter your storage throughput and cache memory layouts. The bottleneck is rarely a simple matter of CPU cycles; it's a complex interplay of memory bandwidth, power density (drawing two to three times what a standard CPU rack requires), and interconnected networking.

| Metric / Concern | Traditional IT Workload | AI Inference Workload |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Bottleneck** | Compute (CPU) or Network I/O | Memory Bandwidth (How fast the GPU reads its own VRAM) |
| **Capacity Sizing** | Scaled linearly by Requests Per Second (QPS) | Dictated by model size, precision, and context length choices |
| **Stack Dependencies** | Software layers isolated from hardware | Model choices completely define networking, storage, and fabric requirements |
| **Power Density** | Standard server rack draw | 2x to 3x traditional power density per rack |
